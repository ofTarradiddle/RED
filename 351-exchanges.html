Section 351 Exchanges

Understanding tax-free exchanges for ETF creation and redemption units

What is Section 351?

Section 351 of the Internal Revenue Code provides for tax-free exchanges when property is transferred to a corporation in exchange for stock, provided certain conditions are met. For ETFs, this provision is critical to the creation and redemption process, allowing Authorized Participants (APs) to exchange baskets of securities for ETF shares (and vice versa) without triggering immediate tax consequences.

This tax-efficient structure is one of the key advantages of ETFs over traditional mutual funds, enabling investors to benefit from the creation/redemption mechanism while maintaining favorable tax treatment.

Section 351 Requirements

For an exchange to qualify as tax-free under Section 351, the following conditions must be met:

  • Control Requirement: The transferors must be in "control" of the corporation immediately after the exchange. Control is defined as owning at least 80% of the total combined voting power and at least 80% of the total number of shares of all other classes of stock.
  • Property for Stock: The exchange must involve property (securities) transferred solely in exchange for stock (ETF shares). Cash or other property received in addition to stock may disqualify the exchange.
  • No Boot: The exchange must be solely for stock. If the transferor receives "boot" (cash or other property in addition to stock), gain (but not loss) may be recognized to the extent of the boot received.

Application to ETF Creation/Redemption

Creation Units

When an AP creates ETF shares, they deliver a basket of securities (the "creation basket") to the ETF in exchange for a specified number of ETF shares (typically 25,000 or 50,000 shares, known as a "Creation Unit"). This exchange generally qualifies as a Section 351 exchange, meaning:

  • The AP does not recognize gain or loss on the exchange
  • The AP's basis in the ETF shares equals their basis in the securities transferred
  • The holding period of the ETF shares includes the holding period of the securities transferred

Redemption Units

When an AP redeems ETF shares, they deliver a Creation Unit of ETF shares to the ETF in exchange for the redemption basket of securities. This exchange also generally qualifies as a Section 351 exchange, with similar tax consequences:

  • The AP does not recognize gain or loss on the exchange
  • The AP's basis in the received securities equals their basis in the ETF shares
  • The holding period of the received securities includes the holding period of the ETF shares

Tax Benefits for ETF Investors

The Section 351 structure provides significant tax advantages for ETF investors:

Tax Efficiency

ETF shares can be created and redeemed without triggering taxable events at the fund level, unlike traditional mutual funds which may realize capital gains when investors redeem shares.

In-Kind Redemptions

APs typically receive securities (not cash) when redeeming, which means the ETF doesn't need to sell portfolio holdings to meet redemptions, avoiding capital gains distributions.

Basis Preservation

The tax basis of securities is preserved through the exchange, allowing for proper tax treatment when the securities are ultimately sold.

Flexibility

The creation/redemption mechanism allows APs to arbitrage price differences between the ETF's market price and its NAV, keeping the ETF trading close to its intrinsic value.

Important Considerations

Custom Baskets

Some ETFs allow for custom baskets (non-pro-rata slices of the portfolio) for creation/redemption. While these may still qualify for Section 351 treatment, the specific terms and conditions should be carefully reviewed to ensure compliance.

Cash-in-Lieu

When certain securities cannot be delivered (e.g., due to corporate actions or trading restrictions), the ETF may provide cash-in-lieu. This cash component may be treated as "boot" and could result in gain recognition to the extent of the cash received.

Individual Investors

Section 351 exchanges are primarily relevant to APs who create and redeem Creation Units. Individual investors who buy and sell ETF shares on the secondary market are subject to normal capital gains tax rules, not Section 351. However, they still benefit from the tax efficiency of the ETF structure.

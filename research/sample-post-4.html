6 — Theoretical Foundation

The Innovation Factor: Endogenous Growth Theory Meets Asset Pricing

The innovation factor operationalizes endogenous growth theory (Romer, 1990; Aghion & Howitt, 1992) within an asset pricing framework. We model innovation as a knowledge production function where R&D capital generates increasing returns through knowledge spillovers and learning-by-doing.

Theoretical Model

Knowledge Production: Ai,t = f(RDi,t, Ki,t-1, Si,t)
where A = innovation output, RD = R&D capital, K = knowledge stock, S = spillovers

Asset Pricing: E[Ri,t+1] = α + βMKTMKTt + βINNINNi,t + εi,t

  • R&D Capitalization: Lev & Sougiannis (1996) show R&D capital generates 20-30% annual returns, yet GAAP expensing creates systematic undervaluation.
  • Patent Quality: Hall, Jaffe & Trajtenberg (2005) demonstrate patent citations predict future market value beyond raw patent counts.
  • Innovation Persistence: Hirshleifer, Hsu & Li (2013) find 4.2% annual alpha after Fama-French 5-factor controls (t-stat: 3.8).

10c — Mispricing Source

Information vs. Structural Mispricing: Why the Anomaly Persists

The innovation factor premium persists because it stems from structural rather than informational mispricing. Structural mispricing cannot be arbitraged away because it arises from accounting conventions, institutional constraints, and behavioral biases.

Sources of Structural Mispricing

  • GAAP Expensing: R&D is expensed, not capitalized, creating accounting distortions that persist regardless of information availability
  • Uncertainty Discounting: Markets systematically discount uncertain future cash flows from innovation, even when information is public
  • Short-Termism: Institutional investors' focus on quarterly earnings creates systematic undervaluation of long-horizon innovation
  • Analyst Coverage: R&D-intensive firms receive less analyst coverage, creating information processing gaps

Management quality in the numbers

The Paper Trail

"Management quality" sounds subjective. It's the kind of thing analysts wave their hands about when they can't find a clean metric. But that's lazy. Capital allocation decisions—acquisitions, buybacks, dividends, capex—leave a paper trail. You can measure them. You can compare them across time and across peers. The outputs are in the financial statements.

Take acquisitions. How many deals has a company done? What did they pay? What happened to the acquired businesses? Did margins improve or deteriorate? Did the company overpay at the top of cycles? You can build a simple scorecard. Most acquirers destroy value. A few consistently create it. The difference is measurable.

Same with buybacks. Did the company buy when the stock was cheap or when it was expensive? You can compare the average price paid to the stock's trading range. Some management teams are disciplined—they buy when their stock trades below intrinsic value. Others buy high because they have nothing else to do with the cash. The data tells the story.

It Isn't Subjective

CEOs who consistently deploy capital at returns above their cost of capital are rare. The market underprices them because "management quality" sounds like a soft factor. It isn't, if you measure the outputs. The allocators who pass the test tend to keep passing it. The ones who fail tend to keep failing.

Dividends and Discipline

Dividend decisions are another signal. A company that pays out 80% of earnings when it has high-return reinvestment opportunities is misallocating capital. One that hoards cash when it has no good uses is equally guilty. The right payout ratio depends on the business—but you can assess whether management is making that trade-off consciously or reflexively.

The best allocators have a clear framework. They know their cost of capital. They only deploy when returns exceed it. They're patient when opportunities don't exist. They're aggressive when they do. That discipline shows up in the numbers over time. Companies that allocate well tend to compound capital. Companies that don't, don't.

The market often treats "quality management" as a narrative. It's not. It's a track record. Build the scorecard. Follow the paper trail. The allocators with the edge are the ones worth owning.

Bottom Line

Capital allocation is measurable. Acquisition success rates, buyback timing, dividend discipline—the outputs are in the financial statements. CEOs who deploy capital well are rare. The market underprices them because it treats management quality as subjective. It doesn't have to be.

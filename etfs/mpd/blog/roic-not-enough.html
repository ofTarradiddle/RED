ROIC Is Not Enough

A company with 40% ROIC that can only reinvest 10% of earnings is not a compounder—it's a cash cow. The magic is ROIC multiplied by reinvestment rate.

February 2026
8 min read
ROIC × r

The compounding equation

The Cash Cow Trap

Investors love high return on invested capital. And they should. ROIC tells you how efficiently a business converts capital into profits. A company earning 40% on every dollar invested is doing something right. But here's the trap: high ROIC alone doesn't create compounding. It creates cash.

A business that earns 40% ROIC but can only reinvest 10% of its earnings back into the business at similar returns is not a compounder. It's a cash cow. The rest of that capital either sits idle, gets returned to shareholders, or—worse—gets deployed at mediocre returns. The math is simple: compounding requires both a high return on capital and a meaningful reinvestment rate. ROIC × r. Both matter.

The market often conflates high ROIC with compounding potential. It's an easy mistake. Companies with dominant market positions frequently earn exceptional returns. But if there's nowhere to put the money—if the reinvestment runway is short—those returns don't compound. They distribute.

The Two Dimensions

Companies like Visa and Constellation Software score high on both dimensions: exceptional ROIC and the ability to reinvest a meaningful portion of earnings at similar returns. That's the compounding sweet spot. Most businesses excel at one or the other. Few excel at both.

The Reinvestment Runway

The real bottleneck for most high-ROIC businesses isn't the return—it's the runway. How much capital can they deploy at attractive returns before they hit diminishing marginal returns? Some industries have almost unlimited runway: software, payments, certain platform businesses. Others hit a wall quickly: regulated utilities, mature consumer brands, businesses with natural geographic or product limits.

When you're screening for compounders, don't stop at ROIC. Ask: where does the capital go? What's the reinvestment rate? Is there room to grow the capital base without sacrificing returns? The companies that pass that test are rare. They're also the ones that compound wealth over decades.

The market underprices this distinction. It rewards high ROIC. It doesn't always reward the combination of high ROIC and reinvestment runway. That's the opportunity. Find the businesses that have both, hold them, and let the math work.

Bottom Line

ROIC tells you how well a business uses capital. Reinvestment rate tells you how much of that capital can be put back to work at similar returns. Compounding requires both. The market often misses the second half of the equation.

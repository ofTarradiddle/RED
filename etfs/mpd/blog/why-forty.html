Why Forty Holdings, Not Four Hundred

The quality tail

The Quality Tail

Forty holdings is concentrated by ETF standards. Most broad-market funds hold hundreds or thousands of names. The logic is diversification: spread risk across many companies so that no single failure can sink the portfolio. But diversification has diminishing returns. The first twenty holdings add a lot of risk reduction. The next twenty add a bit more. The next two hundred add almost nothing.

Meanwhile, going from 40 to 400 holdings meaningfully dilutes the portfolio's return on capital profile. You're no longer adding companies that clearly pass the compounder test. You're adding companies that barely pass the bar—or don't pass it at all. The average quality of the portfolio drops. The average return on capital drops. You've traded concentration risk for a lower expected return. That's not always a good trade.

Forty holdings is sufficient for the quality tail. Beyond the fortieth-best compounder, you're diluting. The marginal company adds more risk than it removes—not because it's volatile, but because it's mediocre. It's a company that earns 12% on capital when you could own one that earns 25%. Over time, that difference compounds.

Diminishing Returns

Going from 40 to 400 holdings adds trivial risk reduction but meaningfully dilutes the portfolio's return on capital profile. The math is clear: the best compounders are rare. The fortieth-best is still excellent. The four-hundredth-best is not. At some point, you're adding names for the sake of diversification, not for the sake of returns.

Concentration and Conviction

Concentration requires conviction. You can't hold forty names and treat them all the same. You need to believe each one earns its place. That discipline forces you to be selective. It forces you to only own companies that pass a high bar. The bar for the fortieth holding is still high. The bar for the four-hundredth is not.

The best compounders are rare. There aren't four hundred of them. There might not even be fifty. The goal is to own as many as you can find that meet the criteria—high ROIC, reinvestment runway, capital allocation discipline—without diluting with mediocre businesses. Forty is a reasonable cutoff. It's concentrated enough to matter. It's diversified enough to avoid single-stock risk. It's the sweet spot for quality.

The market often conflates diversification with safety. It's not that simple. A portfolio of forty excellent businesses is not riskier than a portfolio of four hundred mediocre ones. It might be safer. The companies in the forty are more likely to survive, to compound, to earn returns. The companies in the four hundred include many that will not. Concentration in quality is a strategy. Diversification in mediocrity is not.

Bottom Line

Forty holdings is concentrated by ETF standards but sufficient for the quality tail. Beyond the fortieth-best compounder, you're diluting with companies that barely pass the bar. Diversification has diminishing returns—going from 40 to 400 adds trivial risk reduction but meaningfully dilutes the portfolio's return on capital profile.

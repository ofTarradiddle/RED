The Investment Case

Why a systematic allocation to the world's largest companies is a defensible portfolio construction decision—not a bet, not a prediction, but a position with asymmetric regret.

The Problem with Diversification

Diversification is the only free lunch in finance. This is the most famous insight in all of portfolio theory, and for good reason—it's been empirically validated for decades. But there's a footnote that nobody reads: diversification works best when risk is distributed across many independent sources. When it isn't, the free lunch gets expensive.

The empirical trend in global equity markets is unambiguous: risk and return are concentrating in fewer and fewer companies. The top 10 firms now account for over 20% of global market capitalization. The top 111 account for over half. The bottom several thousand? Noise.

If you hold a broad global index with 3,000+ names, most of your capital is allocated to companies that contribute negligible amounts to the portfolio's total return. You are, in effect, paying for diversification you're not receiving.

Network effects, data moats, platform economics, and regulatory barriers create self-reinforcing advantages for the largest companies. Unlike prior eras of concentration—where size was driven by commodity prices or financial engineering—today's mega-caps have structural moats that compound over time.

We don't predict that concentration will continue. We observe that it has been continuing for decades, that the structural drivers are strengthening, and that most portfolios are implicitly betting against this trend by spreading capital across thousands of names that contribute nothing to the outcome.

The Position, Not the Prediction

DAM1 owns the 111 largest companies by market capitalization, globally. Weighted pro rata. Rebalanced periodically. Subject to standard eligibility, liquidity, and share availability screens. That's it.

This is not stock picking. There is no discretionary overlay. No sector bets. No thematic tilts. The methodology is rules-based and transparent: own the largest, weight by size, rebalance, repeat.

The Regret Framework

If concentration continues:

DAM1 captures the upside directly. A broad index dilutes it across thousands of names. The difference compounds over time. Your allocation to DAM1 is the right call.

If concentration reverses:

You still own the 111 largest, most liquid companies on earth. You underperform a broad index by the spread between mega-cap and smaller companies. The cost is bounded and moderate.

The payoff is asymmetric: large upside in the concentration scenario, moderate downside in the reversal scenario. The regret profile favors holding this position.

Who This Is For

DAM1 is for investors who take concentration risk seriously. Not as a certainty—nobody knows if the current trend persists—but as a non-trivial possibility that deserves a dedicated allocation.

It's for allocators who want a systematic, low-cost way to express the view that the world's largest companies may continue to capture a disproportionate share of global economic value.

It's for anyone who has looked at the Herfindahl index of global equity markets over the past 20 years and thought: "I should probably own more of the winners."

It is not a substitute for diversification. It is a hedge against the scenario where diversification fails.

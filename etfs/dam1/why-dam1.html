The Investment Case

Why owning the biggest companies on earth for 11.1 basis points might be the most reasonable thing in your portfolio.

Diversification Has a Problem

I love diversification. Everyone should diversify. But let's be honest about what's been happening: the top 10 companies are over 20% of global equity market cap. The top 111 are over half. That means if you own a broad index with 3,000 stocks, most of your holdings barely move the needle on your total return. They're just there.

This wasn't always the case. In the 1990s, the top 10 were maybe 8-9%. The increase isn't gradual — it's accelerating. And unlike prior episodes, today's concentration is driven by business models with genuine structural advantages. Network effects. Data scale. Platform lock-in. These don't reverse because somebody writes an op-ed about antitrust.

The question isn't whether you should diversify. You should. The question is whether you also need a dedicated allocation to the possibility that concentration keeps getting worse. We think you do.

These companies aren't big by accident. Their businesses get better the bigger they get. More users means more data means better products means more users. That's a flywheel, and it's very different from Exxon being big because oil costs $80. The structural advantages are real and they compound.

We're not predicting anything. We're looking at a multi-decade trend, noting that the drivers behind it are getting stronger not weaker, and pointing out that most people's portfolios have basically zero dedicated exposure to this risk. That seems wrong to us.

What You're Actually Buying

The 111 largest public companies on earth, weighted by how big they are. We rebalance. We screen for liquidity and investability. There's no secret model, no proprietary signal, no genius stock picking. The market is telling you which companies are biggest. We just own them.

That's boring. Good. Boring is underrated in this business.

Think About It This Way

Concentration keeps going:

You own the winners. Your broad index friends own the winners too, but diluted across 2,900 other things. Over time, that difference matters. A lot.

Concentration reverses:

You lag a broad index. But you still own Apple, TSMC, Novo Nordisk — the biggest, most liquid names in global markets. You're not leveraged. You're not in some exotic structure. The damage is modest.

Big upside in one scenario, small downside in the other. I spend my life looking for setups like this.

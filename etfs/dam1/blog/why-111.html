Why 111? The Arithmetic of Concentration Exposure

How we chose the number of holdings, why it's not 100 or 500, and the empirical basis for a mega-cap cutoff.

December 2025
5 min read

The Short Answer

People ask about the number. Fair enough—it's unusual. Here's the reasoning, which is more empirical than numerological.

We wanted the smallest universe that captures the vast majority of global market capitalization while maintaining sufficient breadth for rebalancing, liquidity, and regulatory comfort. The answer falls in a surprisingly tight range.

The Empirics

Consider the cumulative market cap share as you move down the global rankings:

Companies Included Share of Global Mkt Cap Marginal Gain per Name
Top 10~22%~2.2% each
Top 50~42%~0.5% each
Top 111~55%~0.2% each
Top 250~65%~0.07% each
Top 500~75%~0.04% each

The marginal information gain per additional name drops sharply after roughly 100–120 companies. Beyond that point, you're adding names that contribute fractions of a percent to total market cap. From a concentration-exposure perspective, they're noise.

111 sits at the inflection point. It captures the mega-cap universe comprehensively—every company above approximately $100 billion in market cap—while keeping the portfolio tight enough that its concentration characteristics are meaningfully different from a broad index.

Why Not a Round Number?

100 is arbitrary. So is 111—but at least it's honest about being a calibrated cutoff rather than a marketing-friendly integer. The actual boundary is set by the market-cap distribution, which shifts over time as companies enter and exit the mega-cap tier. 111 reflects the current state of global equity markets. As markets evolve, the methodology adapts.

The Practical Benefits

A 111-name portfolio has several advantages over both narrower (top 30) and broader (top 500) alternatives:

  • Liquidity: Every constituent trades billions of dollars daily. Rebalancing is frictionless.
  • Diversification within concentration: 111 names across multiple sectors and geographies. This isn't a bet on tech alone.
  • Low turnover: The top 111 is remarkably stable. Annual reconstitution typically involves 5–10 name changes.
  • Cost efficiency: 11.1 basis points. Low turnover + liquid names = low implementation costs.

Summary

111 is the answer to a specific question: what is the smallest portfolio that gives you comprehensive exposure to the concentration dynamic in global equities, with the liquidity and breadth characteristics of an institutional-quality product? The math says it's roughly here.

What If Diversification Stops Working?

The core assumption of modern portfolio theory—that diversification is the only free lunch—rests on a world where no single firm dominates. What happens when that assumption weakens?

February 2026
8 min read
Diamond & Diamond Research
111

The number that matters

The Question Nobody Wants to Answer

Diversify broadly, hold the market, rebalance annually. Great advice. I believe in it. I've spent my career on it. But here's what bugs me: that advice assumes returns come from a lot of different places. What if they increasingly don't?

Ten stocks are now over a fifth of all global equity value. That's not a blip. It's been going in one direction for twenty-five years, and the rate is accelerating. If you hold a broad index with 3,000 names, the bottom 2,900 are basically along for the ride. You're paying for diversification that's doing less and less actual diversifying.

I'm not predicting anything. Predictions are for people with more confidence than I have. But I do think the possibility that concentration gets worse deserves more than zero allocation. That's what this fund is.

A Number

In 1980, the ten largest companies were about 9% of global equity market cap. Now it's north of 20%. The top three are worth more than most countries' entire stock markets. You can call that a cycle if you want. I think that's wishful thinking.

"We've Seen This Before"

I hear this a lot. The Nifty Fifty. Japan. Sure. Those reversed. But Exxon was big because oil was expensive. Japanese banks were big because of a real estate bubble. These companies are big because their businesses have network effects that literally get stronger the bigger they get. That's different. I don't think people appreciate how different.

Standard Oil got broken up. The government is barely trying with today's mega-caps, and even if they did, it's not obvious it would work. But I don't actually care about the policy debate. I care about the portfolio question: what happens to your returns if this goes on another ten years and you own 3,000 stocks, 2,900 of which are going nowhere?

The honest answer is you underperform. Not because you did anything wrong, but because your portfolio was built for a world that may not exist anymore.

Two Scenarios, One Portfolio

I think about it this way. There are really only two outcomes:

A

Concentration Continues

The largest firms extend their dominance. Global profits concentrate further. A broad market portfolio increasingly looks like an inefficient way to own what matters. DAM1 outperforms meaningfully.

B

Concentration Reverses

Anti-trust enforcement bites. New competitors emerge. The largest firms' share of profits declines. DAM1 underperforms a broad index—but you still own the 111 largest, most liquid companies on earth. The downside is moderate.

That's an asymmetric setup. In A, you make real money. In B, you lag a bit but you're not blowing up — you own Apple and TSMC and Novo Nordisk, not some levered options position. The downside is boring. The upside isn't. I like that.

What This Actually Is

DAM1 is not a prediction. It's definitely not a tech fund, though there's tech in it because tech companies happen to be enormous. It owns the 111 largest public companies on the planet, weights them by market cap, rebalances, and charges you 11.1 basis points. That's the whole thing.

You don't need to believe concentration is destiny. You just need to think it's possible enough that having zero dedicated exposure to it seems like the wrong answer. I think that's a pretty low bar.

Bottom Line

Keep diversifying. I'm not telling you to stop. But maybe don't bet your whole portfolio on the assumption that the last twenty-five years of increasing concentration just magically reverses. A small allocation to the companies actually winning this game seems like reasonable insurance.

What If Diversification Stops Working?

The core assumption of modern portfolio theory—that diversification is the only free lunch—rests on a world where no single firm dominates. What happens when that assumption weakens?

February 2026
8 min read
Diamond & Diamond Research
111

The number that matters

The Uncomfortable Question

Let me start with what everyone knows but few say out loud: the standard advice—diversify broadly, hold the market, rebalance annually—works beautifully in a world where returns are reasonably distributed across a large number of firms. That world may be ending.

I don't say this lightly. Diversification is perhaps the single most robust insight in all of finance. But its power depends on a structural assumption: that idiosyncratic risk actually exists in meaningful quantities across a large cross-section of securities. When a small number of firms capture an increasingly large share of global profits, revenue, and market capitalization, the "many independent bets" framework starts to fray.

This isn't a prediction. It's a risk assessment. And risks that are hard to diversify away deserve a dedicated allocation.

The Data Point That Should Bother You

In 1980, the ten largest companies by market cap represented roughly 9% of the global equity market. Today, that number exceeds 20%. The top three alone are worth more than most national stock exchanges. This is not mean-reverting noise. This is structural.

Why This Isn't Just a Tech Bubble

The natural objection is: "We've seen concentration before. It reverses." True—sometimes. The Nifty Fifty corrected. Japan's bubble popped. But there's a structural difference this time that's worth taking seriously: network effects, data moats, and platform economics create self-reinforcing advantages that prior eras of concentration didn't have.

Standard Oil was broken up by antitrust. The current mega-caps are being protected by it—or at least not meaningfully constrained. Whether you think this is good or bad policy is irrelevant to the investment question. The question is: what does your portfolio look like if this continues for another decade?

The honest answer for most diversified portfolios: underweight the biggest winners. That's the structural problem DAM1 addresses.

The Asymmetry of Regret

Here's the framework I find most useful. Consider two scenarios:

A

Concentration Continues

The largest firms extend their dominance. Global profits concentrate further. A broad market portfolio increasingly looks like an inefficient way to own what matters. DAM1 outperforms meaningfully.

B

Concentration Reverses

Anti-trust enforcement bites. New competitors emerge. The largest firms' share of profits declines. DAM1 underperforms a broad index—but you still own the 111 largest, most liquid companies on earth. The downside is moderate.

The payoff is asymmetric. In scenario A, the benefit of concentration exposure is large. In scenario B, the cost is bounded—you're not levered, you're not in illiquid names, you're not making a binary bet. You're overweighting the largest companies relative to a cap-weighted global benchmark. The tracking error is manageable. The regret profile favors holding this position.

What DAM1 Is—and Isn't

DAM1 is not a prediction that one company will take over the world. It's not a tech fund. It's not a momentum strategy, though there's overlap. It's a systematic, rules-based exposure to the 111 largest companies globally, weighted by market capitalization within that universe, rebalanced periodically, at a cost of 11.1 basis points.

If you believe concentration risk is real—not certain, but real—then a dedicated allocation to the names most likely to benefit from that dynamic is a reasonable portfolio construction decision. Not a bet. A position.

The Bottom Line

Diversification is still the right default. But defaults should be stress-tested against the scenarios that would make them fail. Concentration is that scenario. DAM1 is the hedge.

The Top 10 Own the Market: A Fifty-Year View

Tracking the share of total global market capitalization held by the ten largest firms, from 1975 to today. The trend is not subtle.

January 2026
6 min read
Diamond & Diamond Research

The Numbers

Start with a simple exercise. Take the ten largest companies by market capitalization at each decade mark and compute their share of the total global equity market. The trajectory is monotonic and accelerating.

Year Top 10 Share of Global Mkt Cap Dominant Sector
1980~9%Oil & Gas
1990~8%Japanese Banks
2000~14%Telecom / Tech
2010~10%Oil / Financials
2020~17%Technology
2025~22%Technology / AI

Two things jump out. First, the 1990 dip was Japan-specific and reversed as the Japanese asset bubble deflated. That was a valuation anomaly, not a structural shift. Second, the post-2010 rise is different in kind: it's driven by companies whose business models create compounding network effects. The more users a platform has, the more valuable it becomes, the harder it is to compete with, the larger it grows. This is not the same dynamic as Exxon being big because oil is expensive.

What This Means for Portfolio Construction

If you hold a global market-cap weighted index, you already have significant exposure to the top 10. But "significant" isn't the same as "optimal if you believe concentration continues." A standard global index holds thousands of names. The bottom 90% of those names are a drag on returns in a concentrating market.

DAM1 doesn't hold the bottom 90%. It holds 111 names—the 111 largest—and nothing else. The result is a purer expression of the concentration thesis at a cost of 11.1 basis points.

The Takeaway

Concentration isn't a prediction. It's a measured, observable, multi-decade trend. Whether it continues is unknowable. Whether you should have exposure to the possibility is a portfolio construction question with a defensible answer.

Borders Don't Matter: Global Concentration Is the Real Story

US-centric investors underestimate how concentration is a global phenomenon. TSMC, Novo Nordisk, ASML—the largest firms transcend geography.

November 2025
7 min read

The Home Bias Problem

Most American investors think about concentration as a US phenomenon. "The Magnificent Seven." "Big Tech." And sure, seven of the ten largest companies are US-listed. But that framing misses the point.

TSMC fabricates the chips that make those US companies valuable. ASML makes the machines that make the chips. Novo Nordisk is reshaping metabolic health globally. SAP runs the back offices of most Fortune 500 companies. Samsung, LVMH, Toyota—these aren't minor players. They're critical nodes in the global economic network.

If you only own the S&P 500, you have no direct exposure to any of them. If the concentration thesis is global—and the data says it is—then your concentration exposure needs to be global too.

A Useful Thought Experiment

If a single company dominated the world economy, it might not be American. It might be the company that controls the most critical chokepoint in the global supply chain. Today, that's arguably TSMC. A US-only portfolio would miss it entirely.

The Non-US Names That Matter

Let me be specific about why global coverage matters. Consider three companies that are not in the S&P 500 but are in DAM1:

TSMC (Taiwan)

Fabricates over 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors. Every AI chip that powers the models everyone is excited about runs on TSMC silicon. If one company is the bottleneck of the digital economy, it's this one.

ASML (Netherlands)

The only company on earth that makes EUV lithography machines—the equipment required to manufacture advanced chips. TSMC can't function without ASML. Neither can Samsung or Intel. A monopoly in the literal sense.

Novo Nordisk (Denmark)

Controls the GLP-1 market—the largest new drug class in decades. With Ozempic and Wegovy, it's addressing obesity at pharmaceutical scale. The company is now worth more than Denmark's GDP.

If any of these companies becomes part of the winner-take-most dynamic, a US-only portfolio misses it. DAM1 doesn't.

The Point

Concentration is not a US story. It's a global story told in US dollars. DAM1 captures it where it lives—across all 14 countries where the world's largest companies are domiciled. At 11.1 basis points.

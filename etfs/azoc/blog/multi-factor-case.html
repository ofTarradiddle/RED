The Case for Multi-Factor Investing

Why combining value, momentum, quality, and other signals produces better risk-adjusted returns than any single factor alone.

February 2026
10 min read
Multi

The signals that matter

The Single-Factor Trap

Every investor knows the factors. Value. Momentum. Quality. Low volatility. Each has its moment in the sun, its academic pedigree, its passionate advocates. But here's what the data shows: betting on any single factor is like betting on one card in a deck. Sometimes you win big. Often you don't.

The Fama-French research that launched a thousand value funds showed value works—until it doesn't. Momentum strategies that crushed in the 2000s went silent for years. Quality factors that shine in downturns fade in bull markets. The pattern is consistent: every factor has its regime, and regimes change.

The question isn't whether factors work. They do. The question is whether you want to bet your returns on one factor's timing.

The Evidence

Academic literature consistently shows that multi-factor portfolios deliver superior risk-adjusted returns. Combining uncorrelated signals reduces volatility while maintaining return potential. The math is clear: diversification across factors works the same way diversification across assets does.

Why Combination Beats Selection

Think of it this way. Value investors are right—until value goes into a five-year drought. Momentum traders are right—until momentum crashes. Quality-focused funds are right—until growth stocks run. Each factor captures something real about how markets price risk and reward. But no single factor captures everything.

Multi-factor investing isn't about picking the best factor. It's about acknowledging that you can't know which factor will work next, so you combine them. You cast a wider net. You let value do its work when value works, momentum when momentum works, quality when quality works. You're not betting on one approach. You're betting on the idea that combining imperfect signals beats relying on perfect timing.

This isn't theory. It's what the data shows. Portfolios that combine value, momentum, quality, and other signals consistently show lower drawdowns and smoother returns than single-factor strategies. The Sharpe ratios improve. The worst-case scenarios get less bad.

The Alchemy Metaphor

Medieval alchemists searched for azoth—the universal substance that could transform base metals into gold. They never found it. But in their search, they discovered chemistry. They learned that combining different elements produced reactions neither element could achieve alone.

Multi-factor investing is similar. There's no single factor that's always right. But combining factors produces something neither factor could achieve alone: more consistent returns, lower volatility, better risk-adjusted performance. The alchemy isn't in finding the perfect factor. It's in the combination.

AZOC doesn't bet on value being right, or momentum, or quality. It bets on the idea that combining these signals—and adjusting their weights based on what's been working—produces better outcomes than any single approach.

Bottom Line

Single factors work until they don't. Multi-factor investing acknowledges that reality and builds portfolios that don't depend on one factor's timing. It's not about finding the perfect signal. It's about combining imperfect signals into something better.

Alchemy, Alpha, and the Art of Not Knowing

The parallel between medieval alchemy's search for azoth and modern finance's search for alpha. Both require humility, experimentation, and the willingness to combine many imperfect approaches.

December 2025
6 min read
Azoth

The universal substance

The Search for Azoth

Medieval alchemists spent centuries searching for azoth—the universal substance that could transform base metals into gold. They never found it. But in their search, they discovered something more valuable: chemistry. They learned that combining different elements produced reactions neither element could achieve alone. They learned that the search itself was the point.

Modern finance has its own azoth: alpha. The excess return that can't be explained by risk. The edge that makes you beat the market. Like the alchemists, we search for it. Like the alchemists, we probably won't find a single source of it.

But like the alchemists, we might discover something in the search: that combining imperfect approaches produces something better than any single approach alone.

The Humility of Not Knowing

The alchemists' search failed in its stated goal. They never found azoth. But their failure taught them something: that you can't know everything, so you experiment. You try combinations. You see what works. You build on what works, discard what doesn't, and keep searching.

Multi-factor investing requires the same humility. You can't know which factor will work next. You can't know which signal will be predictive. So you don't bet on one approach. You combine approaches. You cast a wide net. You let multiple signals do their work, and you adjust based on what's actually working.

This isn't about confidence. It's about acknowledging that you don't have perfect knowledge, so you build portfolios that don't depend on perfect knowledge.

The Art of Combination

The alchemists learned that combining elements produced reactions. Copper plus tin produced bronze—stronger than either metal alone. Lead plus sulfur, in the right combination, produced useful compounds. The magic wasn't in finding the perfect element. It was in the combination.

Multi-factor investing works the same way. Value plus momentum produces something neither factor alone achieves: more consistent returns, lower volatility, better risk-adjusted performance. Quality plus low volatility produces defensive characteristics value alone doesn't. The combination is the point.

AZTH doesn't search for the perfect factor. It searches for the right combinations. It combines value, momentum, quality, and other signals. It adjusts their weights based on what's been working. It acknowledges that the search for alpha, like the search for azoth, is about combining imperfect approaches into something better.

The Parallel

Both alchemy and alpha-seeking require the same things: humility about what you don't know, willingness to experiment, and the recognition that combining imperfect approaches often produces better results than betting on one perfect approach.

Why This Matters

Most investing approaches assume you can know things. Value investors assume they can know when value will work. Momentum traders assume they can know when momentum will work. Quality investors assume they can know when quality will work.

Multi-factor investing assumes something different: that you can't know, so you combine. You don't bet on one factor's timing. You bet on the idea that combining factors reduces your dependence on perfect timing.

This isn't about finding azoth. It's about acknowledging that azoth might not exist as a single thing, but that combining approaches might produce something close enough.

Bottom Line

The search for alpha, like the search for azoth, isn't about finding one perfect thing. It's about combining imperfect things into something better. It's about the humility to acknowledge you don't know everything, and the willingness to build portfolios that don't depend on perfect knowledge.

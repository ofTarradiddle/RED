Why Single Factors Fail

Historical examples of factor droughts: value underperforming 2017-2020, momentum crashing in 2009, quality lagging in strong bull markets. The point: no single factor is always on.

January 2026
7 min read
Drought

When factors go silent

The Value Drought

From 2017 through 2020, value investing went through one of its worst stretches in modern history. The Fama-French value factor—the academic foundation for thousands of funds—underperformed the market by wide margins. Value stocks got cheaper relative to growth, and stayed cheap. The factor that had worked for decades stopped working.

Investors who'd built portfolios around value watched growth stocks run while their holdings stagnated. The academic evidence was still there. The long-term data still favored value. But in real time, with real money, value wasn't working. That's what factor droughts look like.

Value eventually came back. It always does. But if you'd needed returns during those years, "eventually" wasn't much comfort.

Momentum's Crash

Momentum strategies had their moment in the 2000s. Then 2009 happened. Momentum crashed harder than almost anything. The factor that had been printing money went deeply negative. Momentum investors learned the hard way that factors aren't just about returns—they're about timing.

The academic literature on momentum is extensive. The factor works. But academic papers don't tell you when momentum will stop working, or for how long. In 2009, momentum stopped working at exactly the wrong time.

Momentum recovered. Factors always do. But the recovery came after momentum investors had already taken the hit.

Quality in Bull Markets

Quality factors—companies with strong balance sheets, high returns on equity, stable earnings—work beautifully in downturns. They're defensive. They hold up when markets fall. But in strong bull markets, quality often lags. Growth stocks run, and quality stocks—often more mature, more stable—don't keep up.

Quality investors watch bull markets from the sidelines. Their factor works, but it works best when markets don't. In the years when markets are strong, quality underperforms. That's not a bug. It's how the factor works.

The pattern is consistent across factors. Each has its regime. Value works until growth runs. Momentum works until it crashes. Quality works until markets get too strong. No single factor is always on.

The Lesson

Factors work. But they work in regimes. Betting on one factor means betting you can time that factor's regime. Multi-factor investing bets on a different idea: that combining factors reduces your dependence on any single factor's timing.

Why This Matters

If you're building a portfolio around a single factor, you're making a timing bet. You're betting that factor will work when you need it to. Sometimes you'll be right. Often you won't.

Multi-factor investing doesn't eliminate factor risk. But it spreads that risk across multiple factors. When value goes into drought, momentum might be working. When momentum crashes, quality might hold up. You're not betting on one factor's timing. You're betting on the idea that combining factors produces more consistent returns.

Bottom Line

Every factor has its moment. Every factor has its drought. The question isn't whether factors work—they do. The question is whether you want to bet your returns on one factor's timing, or whether you want to combine factors and reduce that timing risk.

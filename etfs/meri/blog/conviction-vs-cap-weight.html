Conviction Weighting vs. Market Cap Weighting

The Cap-Weight Contradiction

Market-cap weighting is a bet that the market's current opinion about relative value is correct. If you believe markets are generally efficient, that's fine. But if you're running an active fund, accepting cap weights is a contradiction — you're saying "I have an edge in stock selection but I'll let the market decide my position sizes." MERI weights by conviction. If a $15B European industrial scores better than a $500B US tech name, the portfolio reflects that.

Think about what cap weighting implies. The largest companies get the largest weights. Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA — they dominate not because they're the best opportunities, but because they're the biggest. The market has already decided. You're just along for the ride. That's passive investing. It has a place. But if you're charging active fees and claiming an edge, why would you outsource your most important decision — how much to own — to the market?

Conviction weighting says: our process identifies the best ideas. The best ideas get the largest weights. A $15B European industrial that scores highly on value, quality, and momentum — and passes fundamental review — can have a larger position than a $500B tech giant that barely passes the screen. Size follows conviction, not market cap.

The MERI Approach

We weight by conviction. Our composite score — value, quality, momentum, and fundamental overlay — determines position size. A smaller company with a stronger score gets a larger weight than a larger company with a weaker score. The portfolio reflects our view, not the market's.

When the Market Is Wrong

Markets are often right. But they're not always right. Bubbles happen. Sectors get overvalued. The biggest companies are sometimes the most expensive. Cap weighting loads you up on exactly those names. Conviction weighting lets you underweight them when the process says they don't deserve the size.

The flip side: smaller companies that the market has overlooked can score very well. A $15B European industrial might have better fundamentals, cleaner balance sheet, and more attractive valuation than a $500B US tech name. Cap weighting would give the tech giant 30x the weight. Conviction weighting might flip that. The portfolio reflects where we see the best risk-adjusted opportunities — not where the market has already placed its bets.

The bottom line: if you have an edge in stock selection, use it for position sizing too. Letting the market decide your weights is lazy. MERI doesn't do that.

Bottom Line

Market-cap weighting is a bet that the market's opinion is correct. If you're running an active fund with an edge, accepting cap weights is a contradiction. MERI weights by conviction. The portfolio reflects our view, not the market's.

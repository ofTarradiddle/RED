Fundamental meets systematic

The Myth of Pure Quant

The best quants in the world — Renaissance aside — all engage with fundamental data at some level. They read transcripts. They understand supply chains. They know that a statistical pattern in earnings revisions reflects real fundamental dynamics. The idea that quant and fundamental are opposing philosophies is marketing, not reality. The best process uses both.

Consider the typical quant workflow: screen for value, quality, momentum. Run a risk model. Optimize. The numbers tell you which stocks pass. But why do they pass? A value screen might flag a European industrial trading at 8x earnings. The quant says "cheap." The fundamental question is: cheap relative to what? Is the earnings power sustainable? Is there a cyclical trough or a structural decline? The data alone won't tell you. Someone has to read the 10-K.

The best systematic investors don't outsource that question. They build processes where quant screens identify candidates and fundamental review validates or rejects them. The screen is the filter; the fundamental work is the gate. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

The Integration

MERI's process explicitly marries quant screens with fundamental review. We use value, quality, and momentum as inputs — not as the final answer. Every name that passes the model gets a fundamental sanity check. Transcripts, filings, industry context. The best process uses both.

Earnings Revisions and Reality

Take earnings revisions. Momentum and quality factors often incorporate revision data. Analysts are upgrading; the stock is trending. But what drives those upgrades? Is it genuine fundamental improvement — better margins, new contracts, market share gains? Or is it management gaming expectations, or analysts playing catch-up after a miss? The statistical signal is the same. The fundamental reality can be very different.

The quants who read 10-Ks understand this. They know that a pattern in the data reflects something real, and they want to know what. That curiosity is what separates robust systematic investing from naive factor tilting. The former survives regime changes; the latter gets run over when the factor stops working and you don't know why.

The bottom line: quant and fundamental are not opposing philosophies. They're complementary inputs to a single process. The best systematic investors have always known this. The rest are catching up.

Bottom Line

The best quants read transcripts. They understand supply chains. They know that statistical patterns reflect real fundamental dynamics. The idea that quant and fundamental are opposing philosophies is marketing. The best process uses both.

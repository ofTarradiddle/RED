Filter ≠ Process

Screening vs. factor investing

Which Pond to Fish In

A factor screen tells you which pond to fish in. It doesn't tell you which fish to catch. Using value, quality, and momentum as filters is fundamentally different from building a value ETF or a momentum ETF. We're using factors as inputs to a process, not as the process itself. The distinction matters for drawdown management and regime-change resilience.

Consider a pure value ETF. It tilts heavily toward cheap stocks. When value works, it works. When value doesn't — and value has had long stretches of underperformance — you're fully exposed. The portfolio is the factor. There's no diversification across factor regimes. A quality ETF has the same problem: when quality gets expensive or momentum reverses, the entire portfolio moves in lockstep.

MERI uses value, quality, and momentum as a combined screen. We're not betting on any single factor. We're saying: show us stocks that score well on all three. That's a much smaller universe. It's also a universe that has historically been more resilient. When value struggles, quality and momentum can offset. When momentum reverses, value and quality provide a floor. The factors work together as a filter, not as the portfolio's identity.

Inputs, Not Outputs

The factor screen narrows the universe. Fundamental review and conviction weighting determine what actually goes in the portfolio and at what size. Factors are the starting point — they're not the finish line.

Regime-Change Resilience

The distinction matters for drawdown management. A single-factor portfolio can get crushed when that factor goes out of favor. A multi-factor screen that requires stocks to pass on value, quality, and momentum simultaneously tends to exclude the most extreme factor bets. You're not loading up on the cheapest-of-the-cheap value traps. You're not chasing the purest momentum names that have already run. You're in the intersection — stocks that have some of each.

That intersection is smaller. It's also, historically, more stable. The factor screen is not the portfolio — it's the first gate. What happens after the screen — fundamental review, conviction weighting, risk management — is where the real portfolio construction begins. Get the distinction right, and you build something that can survive regime changes. Confuse the screen with the strategy, and you're just another factor ETF with a different label.

The bottom line: we use factors as inputs. The portfolio is the output of a process that includes factors, fundamentals, and conviction. That's the difference.

Bottom Line

A factor screen tells you which pond to fish in. It doesn't tell you which fish to catch. Using value, quality, and momentum as filters is fundamentally different from building a factor ETF. We're using factors as inputs to a process, not as the process itself.

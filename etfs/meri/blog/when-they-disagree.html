When Quant and Fundamental Disagree

The most interesting moments are when quant and fundamental disagree. The model says buy; the analyst says pass. Or the reverse. The protocol for disagreement is where the real edge is.

When they disagree

Model Says Buy, Analyst Says Pass

The most interesting moments are when quant and fundamental disagree. The model says a stock is cheap, high-quality, and gaining momentum, but the fundamental review finds accounting red flags or a CEO who's empire-building. We don't take the trade. The quant screen did its job — it surfaced a candidate. The fundamental review did its job — it caught what the data missed. The protocol for disagreement is where the real edge is.

This happens more often than you'd think. Value screens love companies with low multiples. But some of those companies are cheap for a reason: deteriorating margins, hidden liabilities, management that's been capital-destroying for years. The numbers look fine. The 10-K tells a different story. When we find that disconnect, we pass. No amount of quant signal overrides a fundamental red flag.

The reverse is equally important. The model downgrades a name — maybe momentum has faded, or valuation has stretched. But the fundamental case sees an inflection the data hasn't captured yet. A new product launch, a market share gain, a restructuring that's starting to show results. We keep it, but size it down. We don't ignore the quant signal; we don't ignore the fundamental case. We reconcile. Smaller position, higher conviction in the thesis, and we watch the data to see if it catches up.

The Protocol

Quant says buy, fundamental says pass → we don't take the trade. Quant says pass, fundamental says buy → we keep it, smaller size. The protocol is explicit. Neither side overrides the other unilaterally. Both get a vote. The edge is in the reconciliation.

Where the Edge Lives

Most funds either run pure quant or pure fundamental. They never have to resolve disagreement because there's only one voice. The problem: pure quant misses what the data doesn't capture. Pure fundamental misses the discipline of systematic screening. The funds that do both — and have a clear protocol for when they disagree — are rare. That's the edge.

The protocol isn't complicated. Quant says no, fundamental says yes → smaller position, watch for data to confirm. Quant says yes, fundamental says no → no position. Both say yes → full conviction weight. Both say no → obviously out. The interesting cases are the disagreements. That's where you avoid value traps the quant would have bought. That's where you hold inflections the quant would have sold. The protocol for disagreement is where the real edge is.

The bottom line: when quant and fundamental disagree, we have a protocol. It's not about one side winning. It's about reconciling two views to make a better decision than either could make alone.

Bottom Line

The most interesting moments are when quant and fundamental disagree. Model says buy, analyst says pass → we don't take the trade. Model says pass, analyst says buy → we keep it, smaller size. The protocol for disagreement is where the real edge is.

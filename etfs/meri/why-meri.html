The Investment Case for MERI

Why neither quant nor fundamental alone is enough — and why 55 basis points buys you the marriage of both.

The False Dichotomy

The investment industry pretends you have to choose: either you're a quant or you're a fundamental investor. Quants think fundamental analysts are storytellers making post-hoc rationalizations. Fundamental analysts think quants are data miners without insight. They're both partially right about the other side.

The truth is that every good fundamental investor uses quantitative inputs (they screen, they rank, they build models), and every good quant benefits from understanding the businesses behind the tickers. MERI starts from the position that this separation is artificial and commercially motivated, not intellectually honest.

What the Quant Side Does

The quantitative screen serves one purpose: remove the 90% of the universe that isn't worth fundamental attention. It ranks on composite factor scores — value (cheap relative to intrinsic worth), quality (high and stable returns on capital), and momentum (the market is beginning to agree).

This isn't factor investing in the pure sense; it's using factors as a filter. The screen doesn't make the final call. It narrows thousands of candidates to roughly 150 that deserve human attention.

What the Fundamental Side Does

Fundamental analysis answers questions the numbers can't: Is this cheap because it's broken, or because the market hasn't figured it out yet? Is management allocating capital for shareholders or for empire? Are the competitive dynamics improving or eroding? Is the growth real or accounting fiction?

The fundamental layer promotes ~45 names from the quant shortlist into the portfolio and assigns conviction-weighted positions. No market-cap weighting. If we think a mid-cap European industrial compounder is a better bet than a US mega-cap, the portfolio reflects that.

The Rebalancing Discipline

Quarterly rebalancing forces the portfolio to stay honest. Quant scores update, fundamental views get challenged by new data. Positions that no longer pass both filters get cut. No "permanent holdings," no sacred cows.

The process is designed to be unsentimental. If the quant screen degrades on a name and the fundamental case weakens, it's gone — regardless of how well the narrative used to sound.

What You're Paying For

55 basis points buys a genuine two-stage process across a global universe. Not a quant fund with a fundamental marketing story. Not a fundamental fund that runs a backtest after the fact. A real blend.

Forty-five names, conviction-weighted, quarterly disciplined. The merriment is real.
